Isoelectric Precipitation of Protein

To perform the isoelectric precipitation of caesin present in milk.

Principle

Isoelectric precipitation of milk protein involves lowering the pH to the isoelectric point of milk protein (casein) i.e around pH 4.6. At this pH, protein's net electrical charge becomes zero causing electrostatic repulsion to cease and leading to minimal solubility, that results precipitation of milk protein.

Theory

Bovine milk contains four types of caseins denoted as αs1- casein, αs2-casein, β-casein, and κ-casein, each of which is the product of specific genes. The caseins, which are synthesized in the mammary gland, are proteins containing ester-bound phosphate and due to their relatively high content of proline, they tend to have very little secondary structure. Proteins are less soluble at their isoelectric point where they have zero net charge and can most easily approach each other with minimal charge repulsion. Since proteins are also less soluble at very low ionic strength, isoelectric precipitation is usually done at very low or no salt. Isoelectric precipitation is a simple and cost effective method that exploits the reduced solubility of proteins at their pI. By adjusting the solution pH to the target protein's pI, the protein becomes electrically neutral, leading to aggregation and precipitation. The precipitate is then isolated via centrifugation or filtration. This method is effective for bulk protein purification, such as in dairy processing (e.g., casein isolation), and is scalable for industrial applications. However, it has poor specificity for proteins with overlapping pI values and may co-precipitate impurities, necessitating additional purification steps. Despite these limitations, isoelectric precipitation remains a popular choice for largescale protein recovery in food science and biotechnology.

Materials Required
  1. Beaker
  2. Hot plate with magnet stirrer
  3. Thermometer
  4. pH meter
  5. Buchner funnel and filter paper
  6. Vacuum filtration setup
  7. Ice bath
  8. Stirring rod
  9. Graduated cylinder
  10. Skimmed milk - 100ml
  11. 0.2N HCl or 5%acetic acid - 50ml
  12. Distilled water
Procedure
  1. The starting material for the production of casein is skimmed milk. The use of skim milk ensures that the fat content of the casein is low enough to minimize flavor defects arising from deterioration of lipids in the dried casein products.
  2. Following destabilization, the insoluble casein is separated from the soluble whey proteins, lactose, and salts, washed to remove residual soluble solids, and then dried.
  3. Casein can be produced in a number of ways, however industrially they are produced either by isoelectric precipitation or proteolytic coagulation.
  4. During isoelectric precipitation, the pH of the skim milk is reduced to the isoelectric point of casein.
  5. The pH decrease can be achieved by addition of a culture of lactic acid bacteria (converting some of the lactose of the milk to lactic acid), or by addition of a dilute mineral (HCl) or organic acid (acetic acid). The pH of skim milk can also be reduced to the isoelectric point of casein by mixing all or part of the milk with an ion-exchange resin at low temperature.
  6. In the manufacture of acid casein, precipitation is accomplished by under-pressure spraying dilute mineral acids (usually HCl) into preheated milk (25-30°C) flowing in the opposite direction until a pH of 4.6 is reached. Subsequently, steam is injected to heat the acidified milk to the precipitation temperature of about 50°C.
  7. As the pH of milk is falling towards the isoelectric point of casein, a casein gel network (the coagulum) is formed, with good waterholding capacity. The coagulum is then pumped from the coagulation vats and cooked by direct steam injection.

In a Laboratory setting, you can follow protocol as given below;

  1. Measure 100.0 ml of skim milk and add into a beaker. If you are not using skim milk, centrifuge the milk using centrifuge tubes to remove the fat. Centrifuge the milk at 4000 rpm at room temperature (25-30 °C) for 20 minutes. When centrifuging non-skim milk, the fat collects on top and should be removed before heating the milk in the next step.
  2. Transfer milk from all the tubes into the beaker and add equal volume of distilled water and stir well. Now check the pH ( milk pH= 6.7). Bring the temperature of the solution to 40°C.
  3. Start adding 0.2N HCl drop by drop into the milk. Keep stirring and monitor the pH using a pH electrode. The milk should start to curdle. If necessary, add more acid drop by drop until the pH reaches 4.6, which is the isoelectric point of casein. (You can also use 5% acetic acid instead of 0.2N HCl).
  4. Remove the mixture from the heat and let it stand for 5 minutes.
  5. Prepare an ice bath and cool the mixture for 10 minutes, stirring occasionally.
  6. Set up the vacuum filtration apparatus with a Buchner funnel and filter paper.
  7. Filter the cooled mixture to separate the precipitated casein from the whey.
  8. Rinse the precipitate with cold distilled water to remove any remaining acid.
  9. Transfer the casein to a pre-weighed filter paper and let it air dry overnight.
  10. Weigh the dried casein and calculate the yield.
Differences Encountered in a Real Laboratory
  1. Calibrate the pH meter with standard buffer before you start the experiment. Make sure the pH meter is working properly.
  2. Rinse pH electrode thoroughly with distilled water, and place it in a 250.0 ml beaker containing approximately 100.0 to 150.0 ml of distilled water.
  3. Make sure that the pH electrode is properly dipped in the storage solution after completion of the experiment.
  4. Always wear personal protective equipment when you are in the lab. When you enter the lab, switch on the exhaust fan and make sure that all the reagents required for the experiment are available. If it is not available, prepare the reagents using the components shown in the reagent preparation.
  5. Always wear chemical splash goggles while working on this experiment.
  6. Wear gloves while handling chemicals.
  7. Use caution when handling hot liquids and equipment.
  8. Even in dilute form, Acetic acid can cause skin irritation. Rinse immediately if contact occurs.
  9. Ensure proper ventilation when working with acid. All liquids used in this experiment should be handled under a fume hood.
  10. Dispose of all waste in the designated containers, as instructed by your instructor.
  11. When finished, thoroughly clean your work area and return all equipment and chemicals to their appropriate places.
  12. Wash your hands before you leave.
